## The Three Pillars

Order: Fix It → Build It → Scale It
Logic: Matches how most clients arrive. They have something broken first. Then something that needs to exist. Then something that needs to grow. Better → New → Repeatable.

---

### Fix It

*Making what exists work better.*

Most people reach out because something isn't working. Leads are stalling. A process takes 10 steps when it should take 3. A founder is spending 20 hours a week on things that should be automated. Operations are slow, expensive, or inconsistent and it's not obvious where the problem actually is.

I dig into the operations, processes, systems, tools, and team workflows. I find where things are breaking down and redesign them. Not a report. Not a recommendation deck. Actual fixes, implemented and working.

**Examples:**
- Mapped a broken lead flow for a 5-person GTM team, analyzed how all their programs and campaigns were siloed, and rebuilt them into a single connected strategy
- Built a time-study system for a founder, identified bottlenecks consuming 20+ hours/week, then automated and restructured workflows to reclaim that time
- Audited a full marketing/sales tech stack, cut redundant tools, rebuilt data flow between the systems that stayed
- Identified where qualified opportunities were dying in handoff between marketing and sales, redesigned the process end-to-end
- Operationalized an agency's back-end processes using EOS framework: admin automation, tool implementation, cross-team workflows

**Typical results:**
- 15-25 hours/week reclaimed for founders and execs
- 20-40% reduction in operational costs
- 30-50% capacity increase (equivalent to 1-2 hires without hiring)
- Revenue recovered from pipeline and process leaks

**Typical engagement:** Scoped project. Usually 30-60 days depending on complexity.

**Reference pricing:** $6,000-$10,000 (one-off) / $4,000-$8,000/mo (ongoing optimization)

### Build It

*Making something new that needs to exist.*

Sometimes the thing you need doesn't exist yet. Off-the-shelf tools don't solve the problem, or building custom is faster and cheaper than configuring enterprise software. You need a system, an app, an automation, a tool that does exactly what your business requires.

I design and build it. Applications, AI systems, automations, integrations, dashboards, internal tools. Purpose-built for your specific problem.

**Examples:**
- Built a fully automated AI-powered newsletter system: ingests data from multiple sources, researches externally, generates content, queues for review on Thursday, sends on Friday. Generating 10-15 monthly demos from it.
- Built that same system for an internal product update newsletter at another company
- Created a custom desktop app for enterprise sales territory mapping with visualization and planning features. Bought the IP back and developed it into a standalone product.
- Architected a RAG system with Slack integration so sales and marketing teams can chat with their entire Google Drive content library in real time. Fraction of the cost of a $35/seat SaaS license.
- Built a custom data intake pipeline connecting Clay, Airtable, and automation platforms for lead enrichment and routing
- Created an automated directory system that ingests information and auto-completes SEO-optimized listings
- Custom dashboards and reporting systems pulling from multiple tools into a single view

**Typical results:**
- 60-80% cost savings vs. enterprise software over 3 years
- 15-40 hours/week of manual work automated
- Capabilities competitors can't easily replicate
- Changes shipped in days, not vendor roadmap quarters

**Typical engagement:** Scoped project. Timeline varies by complexity.

**Reference pricing:** $10,000-$30,000+ (one-off) / $6,000-$12,000/mo (build + optimize + support)

### Scale It

*Making what works repeatable without you.*

You have something that works. A service, a program, a process. But it's delivered manually, inconsistently, or it lives entirely in one person's head. You can't grow it without that person being involved in every delivery. You can't hire for it because there's nothing documented. You can't step away from it because it falls apart.

I shadow your experts, document everything, systematize the delivery, build training materials, and hand it off so your team runs it independently. Manual and custom work becomes repeatable and scalable with clear quality standards.

**Examples:**
- Built complete service delivery frameworks for a marketing agency across multiple service lines: templates, SOPs, documentation, strategy guides, and team training
- Currently productizing a client's delivery services by shadowing their SME through live deliveries, documenting the process in real-time, building SOPs, then training their team to execute
- Created customer onboarding systems with defined entrance/exit criteria and quality gates at each stage
- Built training programs for both internal teams and customer-facing delivery teams
- Implemented AI and automation into delivery workflows to reduce manual steps and improve consistency
- Helped a technical founder translate their product into a customer journey with marketing programs attached

**Typical results:**
- 2-3x revenue capacity without proportional headcount increase
- 30-50% reduction in delivery costs
- Consistent quality across team members
- 20-40 hours/week of founder/expert time reclaimed
- New hires productive in weeks instead of months

**Typical engagement:** Scoped project per service or program line. Usually 60-90 days.

**Reference pricing:** $8,000-$18,000 per service line (one-off) / $6,000-$10,000/mo (multiple lines or ongoing iteration)

AI is part of how I think about every engagement. When I'm fixing, building, or scaling something, I'm always evaluating where AI and automation create leverage. It's not a separate offering. It's built into the approach.

That said, if you're specifically looking to implement AI in your operations, product, or workflows, this is a significant part of what I do:

- AI system architecture and design
- Custom MCP (Model Context Protocol) development
- AI agent design and implementation
- RAG system architecture (retrieval-augmented generation)
- AI-forward operations design: building systems and processes designed by and for AI from the ground up
- Adoption and implementation: getting teams actually using AI effectively, not just experimenting
- Intelligent automation: beyond simple if/then workflows into adaptive systems that learn and improve

I don't plug in tools and call it AI. I architect systems where AI becomes a structural advantage in how your business operates.

**How it shows up across the pillars:**
- Fix It: automating manual processes, building intelligent monitoring, replacing repetitive decision-making with AI-assisted workflows
- Build It: custom AI agents, RAG architectures, MCP servers, AI-native internal tools, intelligent automation pipelines
- Scale It: AI-assisted quality assurance, automated training and onboarding systems, AI-augmented delivery

## Entry Point: "Tell Me What's Going On"

The first step isn't an assessment. It's a conversation about what's not working and what you want it to look like.

**Interactive intake concept:**

Step 1: **What best describes your situation?** (select one)
- Something's broken or inefficient
- I need something built
- Something works but won't scale
- I need ongoing operational support
- Not sure yet. I just know something needs to change.

Step 2: **What's involved?** (select all that apply)
- Process / workflow
- Technology / tools
- AI / automation
- Strategy / architecture
- People / training
- Data / reporting

Step 3: **Tell me more** (free text)
What's going on? What have you tried? What does "fixed" look like for you?

Step 4: **How should I reach you?**
Name, email, preferred contact method

The feel should be: "here's what's going on, let's figure it out." Not a sales form. Not an assessment questionnaire. The start of solving the problem.

## Writing Guidelines (for all site copy from this framework)

- No em dashes. Use periods, commas, or restructure the sentence.
- Tone: clear, direct, confident, helpful, supportive, informative.
- No clichy openers or generic filler.
- No overly technical jargon without context.
- Lead with examples and stories over abstract descriptions.
- Don't be repetitive. Say it once, say it well.
- Avoid consultant-speak. Write like you're explaining it to someone smart who isn't in your industry.
